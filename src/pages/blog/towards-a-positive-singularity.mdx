There is a story we tell about the future that ends in discontinuity. In this story, the arrival of machine intelligence marks a break — the last human invention, after which everything is different in ways we cannot predict. Vernor Vinge called it the Singularity. Ray Kurzweil called it the Rapture of the Nerds. It has the shape of an event horizon: knowable up to a point, then dark.

I want to tell a different story.

Not because I think the discontinuity is impossible — the acceleration of capability over the past decade has been genuinely startling — but because the framing of rupture forecloses something important: the possibility that we can *choose* what kind of transition this is.

## The fossil and the future

When Jonas Salk said that the most important question for humanity was whether we were good ancestors, he was pointing at a specific failure mode of modernity: the tendency to treat the future as a resource to be extracted rather than a commons to be tended.

I've spent forty years building health information systems — first for the VA, then the DoD — and the pattern I keep encountering is what I call *fossil binding*: designing systems that lock in the assumptions of their moment of creation and resist the changes that the future will require. VistA itself has this quality. It was a radical act of openness in 1980; by 2020 it had become a burden.

The alternative isn't better prediction. You can't predict the future well enough to design for it. The alternative is *future binding*: building systems that preserve optionality, that hold their assumptions lightly, that are organized around principles rather than implementations.

## What health taught me about intelligence

The work I'm doing now — building EOH, an agent-based health system organized around a six-dimensional model of human vitality — has clarified something I suspected but couldn't articulate earlier in my career.

Health is not the absence of disease. It is a generative state: the capacity to move toward what matters, to maintain coherent agency through time, to be embedded in meaningful relationships and contexts. A health system that only tracks pathology is like a telescope that only looks for darkness.

The same thing is true of intelligence. What we are building with AI systems is not — or should not be — a machine that maximizes some narrow objective. It should be a system that expands the adjacent possible: that makes more futures available, that helps human beings become better ancestors.

## The choice in front of us

The positive singularity is not inevitable. Neither is the negative one. What's in front of us is a design problem of the first order — and design problems have authors.

This platform is our small contribution to working in public on that problem. Not from the center of the AI industry, but from its edges: a physicist asking what the universe is made of, a systems architect asking what health could become, both of us asking what it means to think carefully about what comes next.

We don't know the answers. We're trying to ask better questions.
